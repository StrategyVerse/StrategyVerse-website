Beyond Impressions: How to Actually Measure PR ROI

Back to Insights
StrategyVerse Content Team 8 min read

The Question Every Indian Business Owner Asks

You have been running a PR programme for six months. Your agency sends you a monthly report filled with numbers. Circulation figures. Impressions. Column centimetres. The numbers look big. But when your CFO asks a straightforward question — what did we actually get for that spend? — you do not have a clear answer.

You are not alone. Across India, from funded startups in Bengaluru to manufacturing firms in Noida, business owners share the same frustration. They believe in the value of public relations. They understand that reputation matters. But they struggle to draw a straight line from PR activity to business results. And when budgets tighten, PR is often the first line item to get questioned because the return feels invisible.

The problem is not that PR lacks value. The problem is that most PR measurement frameworks are still stuck in the 1990s.

The Vanity Metrics Trap

Let us talk about the metrics that dominate most PR reports in India — and why they are misleading.

Impressions. This is the most commonly reported number, and the most meaningless. When your agency says a story generated 4.2 million impressions, they are citing the total readership or viewership of the publication. It does not mean 4.2 million people read your story. It means that many people theoretically could have seen it. The actual number who noticed, read, and remembered your coverage is a tiny fraction of that figure.

Advertising Value Equivalent (AVE). AVE attempts to answer the ROI question by calculating what you would have paid if the earned media coverage had been a paid advertisement. It sounds logical, but it is fundamentally flawed. Editorial coverage and advertising are not the same thing. A well-placed story carries credibility that an ad never will. AVE also ignores tone — a negative article about your company would technically have a high AVE, which makes no sense at all. Most global PR industry bodies have formally rejected AVE as a valid metric.

Clip counts. Counting the number of media placements tells you how active your PR programme is, but not how effective it is. Ten placements in irrelevant trade publications may matter less than one well-crafted story in The Economic Times that your investors and clients actually read.

These metrics are not useless as activity indicators. But they should never be the centrepiece of your PR measurement. They tell you what happened. They do not tell you what it meant for your business.

Five Metrics That Actually Matter

If you want to measure PR in a way that connects to business outcomes, here are the five metrics you should be tracking.

1. Branded Search Volume Lift

When your PR is working, more people search for your company by name. This is one of the clearest signals that earned media is generating awareness and interest. Use Google Trends or Google Search Console to track branded search volume before, during, and after major PR campaigns. If you see a spike in searches for your company name after a story runs, that is direct evidence of PR driving interest.

2. Website Traffic from Media Coverage

Every piece of coverage that includes a link to your website — or prompts someone to search for you — should show up in your analytics. Track referral traffic from publication domains. Monitor the organic search traffic spike on days when coverage goes live. If a story in Mint or Business Standard drives 300 visitors to your website, and 15 of them fill out your contact form, you have a tangible conversion trail from PR activity to lead generation.

3. Share of Voice vs Competitors

Share of voice measures how much of the media conversation in your industry features your brand compared to your competitors. If you are a cleantech company and your competitor gets mentioned in eight out of ten industry stories while you appear in two, you have a share of voice problem regardless of how many placements you are getting in absolute numbers. Tracking this over time shows whether your PR is genuinely increasing your visibility in the spaces that matter.

4. Lead Attribution from PR Touchpoints

This is where most companies fall short, but it is not as complicated as it sounds. When a new lead comes in, ask them how they heard about you. Add a simple dropdown to your contact form with options like "media coverage," "search engine," or "referral." You can also use UTM parameters on links shared in press releases and contributed articles to track exactly which piece of coverage drove which leads. Over time, you will build a clear picture of which PR activities generate the most valuable enquiries.

5. Sentiment Shift Over Time

Volume without context is meaningless. Ten articles that position your CEO as an industry expert are worth more than fifty articles that simply mention your company in a list. Track not just how often you appear in media, but how you appear. Are you being quoted as a thought leader? Are stories using your key messages? Is the overall tone positive, neutral, or negative? Sentiment tracking requires more effort than simple clip counting, but it tells you whether your PR is actually shaping perception in the direction you want.

Setting Up Simple Tracking

You do not need expensive PR analytics software to start measuring properly. Here is a practical setup that any Indian SMB can implement this week.

UTM parameters. Every link you include in a press release, a contributed article, or an email pitch should have UTM tags. Use Google's free Campaign URL Builder. Set the source as the publication name, the medium as "earned-media," and the campaign as the story topic. This way, when someone clicks through from a Times of India article to your website, you can trace that visit precisely in Google Analytics.

Google Analytics goals. Set up goal tracking for the actions that matter — contact form submissions, brochure downloads, demo requests. When you combine this with UTM-tagged links, you can see exactly which media coverage led to which conversions.

Brand mention alerts. Set up Google Alerts for your company name, your CEO's name, and your top two or three competitors. This costs nothing and gives you daily updates on who is being mentioned and where. For more robust monitoring, tools like Mention or Brand24 offer affordable plans suitable for growing businesses.

"How did you hear about us?" field. Add this to every lead intake form, every sales call script, and every new customer onboarding process. It is the simplest and most underused attribution tool available.

Your Monthly PR Dashboard

Here is a practical template you can build in a simple spreadsheet and review each month.

  • Media placements this month: Number of stories, with links and publication names
  • Key message pull-through: How many placements used your core messages or quoted your spokesperson
  • Sentiment breakdown: Positive, neutral, and negative coverage as a percentage
  • Branded search volume: Month-over-month change from Google Trends or Search Console
  • Website traffic from PR: Referral visits from publications plus organic search spikes on coverage days
  • Leads attributed to PR: From UTM tracking and self-reported attribution
  • Share of voice: Your mentions versus top two or three competitors in your sector

This single page will give you more actionable insight than a fifty-page report filled with impressions and AVE numbers. Review it monthly. Look for trends over quarters. Share it with your leadership team so they can see the connection between PR activity and business outcomes.

Why Strategic PR Compounds Over Time

There is one more thing that business owners need to understand about PR measurement, and it is perhaps the most important point of all. PR is not like paid advertising. You do not turn on a campaign on Monday and measure conversions by Friday.

Strategic PR compounds. A story in a major publication builds your credibility. That credibility makes it easier to land the next story. That next story gets shared by an industry influencer. A potential client sees your name three or four times over two months and finally visits your website. They do not fill out a form immediately — they bookmark it. Three weeks later, they come back and request a meeting.

None of that shows up in a single month's report. But over six months, the effect is unmistakable. Your brand becomes a familiar name in your industry. Your sales team finds that prospects already know who you are before the first call. Your recruitment pipeline improves because talented people want to work for companies they have read about.

The businesses that get the most from PR are the ones that measure it properly, stay patient, and give it time to compound. The ones that quit after three months because they did not see an immediate spike in revenue were never really investing in PR. They were renting attention. And rented attention disappears the moment you stop paying.

Start Measuring What Matters

If you are spending money on PR and not tracking these five metrics, you are flying blind. You may be getting excellent results without knowing it. Or you may be wasting your budget on activity that looks busy but achieves nothing. Either way, you deserve clarity.
