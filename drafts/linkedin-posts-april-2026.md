## --- WEEK 1: Tuesday 7 April 2026 ---

**Blog:** Why Every Indian Business Needs a Crisis Communication Plan

**LinkedIn Post:**

I once got a call from a founder at 11 PM. A disgruntled ex-employee had posted something damaging on social media. The post was gaining traction. The founder's first question: "What do we do?"

My first question back: "Do you have a crisis communication plan?"

Silence.

This is more common than you'd think. Fewer than half of all businesses have any kind of documented crisis plan. They assume they'll figure it out when the time comes. But when a crisis actually hits, nobody thinks clearly, everyone has a different opinion on what to say, and by the time leadership aligns on a response, the narrative has already been shaped by someone else.

I've learned over the years that the first 60 minutes after a crisis breaks are what really matter. That small window decides whether you control the story or spend the next three weeks doing damage control.

We put together a practical guide on building a crisis communication plan — the kind you prepare when things are calm, so you're ready when they aren't.

## --- WEEK 2: Tuesday 14 April 2026 ---

**Blog:** Beyond Impressions: How to Actually Measure PR ROI

**LinkedIn Post:**

A few months ago, a client asked me a question that I've heard probably a hundred times in various forms: "We got coverage in three publications last month. What was the ROI?"

I asked what they meant by ROI. They said impressions. The agency they worked with before us used to send a report every month with impressive-looking numbers — 2.3 million impressions, 47 media mentions, AVE of 12 lakhs.

The thing is, none of those numbers told them whether anyone actually read the coverage, visited their website because of it, or eventually became a customer. The numbers looked good in a presentation but meant nothing for the business.

There are better ways to measure whether your PR efforts are actually working — branded search trends, referral traffic from media, lead attribution, share of voice relative to competitors. None of these require expensive tools. Most of it you can track with Google Analytics and a bit of discipline.

We wrote about how to set this up. It includes a simple monthly dashboard framework that any Indian SMB can start using this week.

## --- WEEK 3: Tuesday 21 April 2026 ---

**Blog:** Your Business Is One Google Search Away From a Crisis

**LinkedIn Post:**

Try something right now. Open an incognito browser window and Google your company name. Look at every result on page 1.

What you see there is exactly what your prospective clients, potential hires, and future partners see before they ever pick up the phone or reply to your email.

I recently worked with a company that had been in business for over a decade. Good reputation in their industry, solid client base. But when you Googled them, the third result on page 1 was a two-year-old Glassdoor review from a disgruntled former employee. That single result was costing them talent — candidates were seeing it during their research and quietly dropping out of the hiring process.

The thing about online reputation is that most Indian businesses don't think about it until something goes wrong. By then, you're playing defence. The businesses that handle this well are the ones that proactively build a strong enough digital presence that one bad result can't dominate their story.

## --- WEEK 4: Tuesday 28 April 2026 ---

**Blog:** Why Indian Startups That Tell Stories Win More Trust

**LinkedIn Post:**

I was reviewing a startup's media pitch last week. It opened with their funding amount, followed by a list of product features, and ended with the number of users on their platform.

I asked the founder: why did you start this company?

Turns out he had spent eight years working in logistics and saw firsthand how small manufacturers in tier-2 cities were getting crushed by inefficient supply chains. He quit a well-paying job because he genuinely believed he could fix it. His first customer was a factory owner in Ludhiana who he'd met at a chai stall.

None of this was in the pitch. The most interesting part of his story — the part that would make a journalist actually want to write about him — was completely missing.

This keeps happening. Indian startups default to press releases that read like product spec sheets and then wonder why the media doesn't pick them up. Journalists write stories about people, problems, and purpose. Features and funding rounds are supporting details, not the headline.
